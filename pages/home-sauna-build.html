Building a Finnish Sauna in My Backyard

Six months in Scandinavia, one backyard build in San Francisco. The engineering behind getting löyly right.

This past summer my wife and I spent six months traveling, and somewhere in Scandinavia we developed a sauna habit. Not in a wellness-trend way, but in the way that happens when something is simply part of the infrastructure of daily life. Saunas were in hotel rooms, available at public facilities for a few euros, and treated with the same casualness as a shower. We used one almost every day. We felt great. When we got home, we knew we wanted to keep it going.

The problem is that "sauna" in the US usually means something different from what we'd been experiencing. The American market is dominated by infrared saunas, which use radiant panels to heat your body directly rather than heating the air in the room. They run at lower temperatures and are easy to install. They're not bad, they're just not what we wanted.

What we wanted was löyly. In Finnish sauna tradition, löyly (pronounced roughly "loy-lu") is the act of ladling water over a pile of hot stones, producing a burst of steam that sharply raises the perceived heat in the room. It sounds simple, but it's the center of the whole experience, and getting it right is more constrained than it looks.

Like any engineering problem, a list of requirements was forming in my head:

  • Heater size and stone load The stones need enough thermal mass to flash water into steam on contact. Undersized heaters just get doused.
  • Temperature ceiling A proper Finnish sauna runs 80–100°C. Most infrared saunas top out around 50°C. Löyly doesn't work at that temperature.
  • Bench height relative to the heat pocket Heat stratifies in a sauna room — hot at the top, cold at the floor. Your bench needs to put you in the heat pocket, with your feet above the stones. The Finns call this the first law of löyly. Most North American kits have ceilings too low to achieve it.
  • Room volume Too small and you're sitting on top of the heater getting blasted with radiant heat. You want convective heat — like a hot air bath — which requires enough distance and air volume to develop.
  • Ventilation Fresh air needs to come in while stale, CO₂-heavy air exhausts out. Most gym saunas skip this entirely, which is why they feel suffocating after a few minutes. A properly ventilated sauna, you leave because you've had enough heat, not because you can't breathe.

Kit after kit checked some of these boxes. None checked all of them. At a certain point I was close to designing one myself, until I found Knotty Sauna, a small company out of Canada. I went back and forth with the founder Wesley over a lot of questions. He'd approached it the same way I would have — starting from Finnish first principles and not compromising where it counted. Being a fellow Canadian didn't hurt either.

We ordered the kit and have been putting it together with a good group of friends. The pieces arrive pre-cut, but there's still real assembly involved and I'm enjoying every bit of it. I've always liked woodworking, and there's something satisfying about building something this purposeful with your hands.
